    Background information

    Puzzle description

    Most of you have played the game of Chess. (If not, here is a link ) One of the most interesting pieces on a chessboard is the Knight, whose move consists of jumping from one corner to the diagonally opposite corner of a rectangle of dimensions 3 squares by 2 squares. The Knight can do this even if there are pieces between the corner squares, but it can't land on a piece of the same color. One curious fact about Knights is that a Knight on an empty 8x8 chessboard can make 63 consecutive moves to land on each square of the chessboard exactly once (this includes the square it started on). This is called a "Knight's tour". There are two kinds of Knight's tours: open and closed. A closed Knight's tour means that the Knight makes an extra Knight's move at the end of the tour to end up on the same square it started on. An open Knight's tour doesn't do that.

    It turns out that Knight's tours exist on more than just 8x8 boards; most \(M \times N\) grids with \(M\) rows and \(N\) columns, where \(M\) and \(N\) are both positive, have (at least) open Knight's tours. However, some boards don't. For instance, a 4x4 board has no open or closed Knight's tour, and a 3x4 board has only an open Knight's tour. Any board smaller than 3x4 has no Knight's tour. In addition, some boards only have Knight's tours if the Knight starts off on particular squares. So the problem we will be solving is this:

    Knight's tour: problem statement

    Given an empty board (two-dimensional grid) with \(M\) rows and \(N\) columns, where \(M\) and \(N\) are both positive integers, and a location at row \(P\) and column \(Q\), where \(0 \leq P < M\) and \(0 \leq Q < N\), find an open Knight's tour that starts with the Knight at location \((P, Q)\).

    You might think that this is a difficult search problem, possibly even NP-complete1. However, it turns out that there is an amazingly simple algorithm that can generate Knight's tours in linear time for most boards that are solvable. It is not guaranteed to succeed, but it usually does. (Whether you want to consider this an "algorithm" or not is up to you; some people would just consider it a strategy or heuristic.)

    Outline of the solution algorithm

    The solution algorithm we will be using is called "Warnsdorff's rule". As the name implies, it's less of an algorithm than a heuristic (rule of thumb). It was invented by H. C. von Warnsdorff in 1823. The rule states that a Knight should always move to a square that has the fewest possible subsequent Knight's moves. If there is more than one candidate square with the fewest possible subsequent Knight moves, choose any one of them at random. This simple rule will result in a Knight's tour more than 85% of the time on most boards less than 50x50, even without backtracking! It's rather astonishing that such a trivial rule can be so effective, but you will see that it's true. Even larger boards (100x100 and bigger) can be solved by this rule.

    Running the knights_tour program

    Once you have written the modules described below (and, ideally, they pass all the tests), you can compile the knights_tour program by simply typing:

    $ make

    in the terminal. (We're assuming you are in the directory where the Makefile and all the code you downloaded and edited is located.) Then you can run the program. If you run it without arguments, you just get a usage message:

    $ ./knights_tour
usage: ./knights_tour nrows ncols [-start m n] [-f] [-q]
  nrows: number of rows (int)
  ncols: number of columns (int)
  -start m n: starting square (int, int)
  -f: use functional board
  -q: don't print solution board

    The simplest way to run it is by supplying the number of rows and columns:

    $ ./knights_tour 8 8

       0   1   2   3   4   5   6   7
   +----------------------------------+
 0 |   1  14  29  62  11  16  19  48  |
 1 |  28  63  12  15  30  49  10  17  |
 2 |  13   2  59  50  61  18  47  20  |
 3 |  58  27  64  35  56  31  52   9  |
 4 |   3  38  57  60  51  46  21  32  |
 5 |  26  41  36  55  34  53   8  45  |
 6 |  37   4  39  24  43   6  33  22  |
 7 |  40  25  42   5  54  23  44   7  |
   +----------------------------------+

Valid solution found!

    Note that row numbers increase going from top to bottom and column numbers increase going from left to right. The row number is printed out on the left side of the board, while the column number is printed out above the board.

    This Knight's tour starts at location (0, 0) (row 0, column 0); this is the default. This location is the first Knight placement, so a 1 is printed out at that location. We call this number the index of the solution. The next Knight is at location (2, 1), which has index 2, and the next is at (4, 0), which has index 3, and so on. The last location of the Knight's tour is at (3, 2), which has index 64, of course. This Knight's tour is not closed (most of them won't be). The program also checks that the solution is valid; if it isn't, it will fail.

    We can also specify the starting location:

    $ ./knights_tour 8 8 -start 1 2

       0   1   2   3   4   5   6   7
   +----------------------------------+
 0 |   2  35   4  19  46  33  14  17  |
 1 |   5  20   1  34  15  18  49  32  |
 2 |  36   3  54  45  56  47  16  13  |
 3 |  21   6  37  58  53  50  31  48  |
 4 |  38  59  52  55  44  57  12  27  |
 5 |   7  22  63  60  51  28  43  30  |
 6 |  64  39  24   9  62  41  26  11  |
 7 |  23   8  61  40  25  10  29  42  |
   +----------------------------------+

Valid solution found!

    Note that not all boards are solvable:

    $ ./knights_tour 4 4
Solution not found :(

    Also, some boards are only solvable when started from particular squares, and the algorithm may not work every time even if the board is solvable. But if it's solvable and the board isn't too large, it should work most of the time.

    There are two other command-line options. The -q option suppresses the board printout and just tells you whether a solution was found or not. The -f option uses the functional board representation; the default is the imperative board representation. These options can be combined.

    Our code can generate Knight's tours for small boards (8x8 and smaller) very quickly (less than a second) if the board is solvable at all. The algorithm doesn't always succeed (and the success rate goes down as boards get larger), but if your code rarely generates solutions for 8x8 boards, or takes a very long time to find a solution, you are probably doing something wrong.

    We recommend that you concentrate on making your code pass the test scripts for the individual modules we describe below. Only try the knights_tour program after all the test scripts pass.

    [OPTIONAL] Running the interact program

    To compile the interactive program, do this:

    $ make interact

    Then run the program like this:

    ./interact

    The program will prompt you to enter the number of rows and columns:

    Enter number of rows and columns:

    We'll enter 3 for rows and 4 for columns. (This is actually the smallest solvable board).

    Enter number of rows and columns: 3 4

    The program will print out two 3x4 grids and a prompt:

    Enter number of rows and columns: 3 4

       0   1   2   3
   +------------------+
 0 |   .   .   .   .  |
 1 |   .   .   .   .  |
 2 |   .   .   .   .  |
   +------------------+

       0   1   2   3
   +------------------+
 0 |   2   3   3   2  |
 1 |   2   2   2   2  |
 2 |   2   3   3   2  |
   +------------------+


interact>

    The top grid is for the solution indices. The first Knight placed would be at index 1, the next at index 2, etc. Since no Knights have been placed, the board is empty (we'll see what this means below).

    The bottom grid is the "reachability" grid. Each location in this grid gives the count of possible Knight's moves starting from that location. Only valid Knight's moves are allowed i.e. moves on the board, but not moves landing on a previously-placed Knight. If a location has a Knight on it, the grid will show a dot (.). This grid shows us that there are 2 knight's moves starting from location (0, 0), 3 from (0, 1), 3 from (0, 2), etc. You can use this to help you figure out which location to place a Knight in next (assuming you want to use Warnsdorff's rule). The automated solver will use this grid as well, as we will see.

    The prompt is interact>. There are only three kinds of commands you can give:

    • q quits the program.
    • u undoes the last move. If there was no last move, nothing happens.
    • Otherwise, enter the row and column indices where you want to place the knight. It will check for errors and place it if there are none.
